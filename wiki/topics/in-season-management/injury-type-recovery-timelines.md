## Summary

Injury recovery timelines are governed by tissue biology (ligament, tendon, and muscle heal on different clocks), not by a single "weeks missed" figure, and every sampled model converges that the correct fantasy question is not when a player is medically cleared but when he reaches functional return (can perform the position's required movements) and fantasy return (receives normal role and usage). Across the four injury types most common to skill positions — ACL tears, hamstring strains, high ankle sprains, and MCL sprains — sources converge on a rough severity hierarchy (ACL longest and most fantasy-damaging, followed by high ankle sprain, then hamstring, with isolated low-grade MCL sprains generally the most recoverable) but diverge meaningfully on exact week-count ranges, so this page treats specific timelines as directional priors rather than settled figures. Running backs and wide receivers are consistently identified as the positions most exposed to lower-body injury because their production depends on repeated high-force acceleration, deceleration, and cutting, while pocket quarterbacks are the least exposed and mobile quarterbacks fall closer to the skill-position risk profile.

## Core Knowledge

### Four distinct return thresholds, not one

Sources converge that fantasy managers routinely collapse four separate events into one: medical return to play (cleared to participate), functional return (can perform the position's required movements at game speed), fantasy return (receiving normal snaps, routes, touches, and red-zone opportunity), and full pre-injury production (efficiency and role both restored). A player can clear the first threshold by a wide margin before reaching the third or fourth. Treating "active" or "off the injury report" as equivalent to "back to normal value" is named across sources as the single most common injury-related fantasy error.

### ACL reconstruction is the longest and most fantasy-damaging injury class

Sources converge that ACL reconstruction requires roughly 9-12 months before return to NFL game action, with functional recovery (full trust in cutting, deceleration, and contact) frequently lagging the first game back by several additional months — one source places true functional restoration as late as 12-18 months post-surgery, with underlying tissue maturation continuing even longer. Sources diverge on precise week-by-week figures but agree directionally that the first returning season carries substantially more downside variance than the second. Running backs are consistently flagged as the most affected position because their value depends on burst, lateral cutting, and contact tolerance — traits most directly compromised by ACL reconstruction and slowest to return to full confidence. Wide receivers whose value depends on sharp route breaks and separation are similarly exposed, though possession or size-based receivers who win through catch radius rather than separation may be less affected than separation-dependent receivers. Tight ends fall in between, since route demands vary more by role than for receivers. Quarterbacks are the least affected as a group, but the effect is highly playing-style dependent: pocket passers can return with comparatively little fantasy impact once medically cleared, while quarterbacks whose value depends on scrambling and designed runs can lose a large share of fantasy floor even with fully intact passing mechanics.

An ACL injury should not be treated as a single uniform severity class. Associated damage — meniscus tear, collateral ligament involvement, cartilage damage, or a second surgical procedure — meaningfully extends both the medical and fantasy recovery timeline, and a player with an isolated ACL tear should not be placed in the same projection bucket as a player with ACL-plus-meniscus or ACL-plus-MCL damage. A minority of sources further distinguish by graft type (hamstring autograft, patellar tendon autograft, quadriceps autograft, or allograft), with directional agreement that graft choice shifts the biologic-incorporation and reinjury-risk profile somewhat — this distinction is corroborated by more than one source but not strongly enough across the full panel to treat as settled, and specific week-count deltas by graft type should not be trusted without independent verification.

### Hamstring strains are shorter but carry the highest recurrence risk

Sources converge on a graded severity scale: mild strains generally return in roughly 1-3 weeks, moderate strains in roughly 3-6 weeks, and severe or high-grade strains (including proximal tendon involvement) in 6-12 or more weeks — figures that vary somewhat by source but agree on the relative ordering. The dominant fantasy risk is not the initial absence but recurrence: hamstring strains have a well-documented pattern of re-aggravation when a player returns before full high-speed sprint capacity is restored, and an early return (particularly one occurring within roughly two weeks of the original strain) is a flagged recurrence risk across sources. Because official practice-participation labels report only whether a player practiced (Full, Limited, Did Not Participate) and not the percentage of reps or peak velocity reached, whether a returning player has regained 80% or 100% of functional sprint capacity is genuinely difficult to determine from public reporting.

Wide receivers and running backs are the most exposed positions because both depend on repeated high-velocity acceleration; a receiver can return to normal snap counts while quietly avoiding maximum-speed vertical routes, and a running back can retain a normal carry count while producing fewer explosive runs. Tight ends are somewhat less exposed than receivers because route demands vary more, and quarterbacks are affected mainly to the extent their value depends on scrambling or mobility rather than pocket mechanics.

### High ankle sprains create a longer and more variable fantasy drag than the "weeks missed" figure suggests

A high ankle sprain (syndesmotic injury, involving the ligaments connecting the tibia and fibula above the ankle joint) is a distinct and generally more severe injury than an ordinary lateral ankle sprain, and treating the two as equivalent is flagged as a common and analytically weak practice. Timelines vary by severity: mild stable injuries in roughly 2-4 weeks, moderate injuries in roughly 4-8 weeks, and severe or unstable injuries — including those requiring surgical stabilization — extending well beyond 8 weeks, sometimes several months for surgical cases. Even when a player is fully active and posting a normal-looking snap count, sources converge that push-off strength, first-step burst, and change-of-direction ability are commonly suppressed for a meaningful window after return, meaning role and volume can normalize well before efficiency does. This makes the high ankle sprain a specific case where "active" and "back to normal" diverge more than for most other injury types.

Running backs and wide receivers are the most affected positions because plant-and-drive mechanics and cutting are directly load-bearing through the ankle. Quarterbacks are affected primarily through pocket movement, throwing-base stability, and play-action footwork; mobile quarterbacks carry a materially larger fantasy penalty than pocket passers. Tight ends are affected through both route separation and blocking leverage.

### MCL sprains are generally the most recoverable of the four injury classes when isolated

MCL injuries are graded I through III by severity, with Grade I (mild) typically returning in roughly 1-2 weeks, Grade II (partial tear) in roughly 2-4 to 6 weeks, and Grade III (complete tear) in roughly 4-8 or more weeks. Sources converge that the MCL generally heals more reliably than the ACL because of its blood supply and anatomical location, and that isolated, lower-grade MCL sprains are usually the least fantasy-damaging injury in this comparison set — provided the injury is not accompanied by meniscus, cartilage, or ACL damage, which meaningfully changes the projection. The primary functional limitation is valgus (inward-knee) stability, affecting a runner's ability to cut off the outside foot, a receiver's release and cutting against press coverage, and a quarterback's plant-and-throw mechanics. Grade and associated damage matter far more than the "MCL" label alone; a Grade III MCL sprain with instability is a materially different projection case than a Grade I sprain.

### A practical severity hierarchy, held loosely

Synthesizing across sources, a rough fantasy-relevance severity ordering (most to least damaging) is: ACL reconstruction, especially for running backs and explosive receivers; severe or surgical high ankle sprain; recurrent or high-grade hamstring strain; Grade II-III MCL sprain, particularly with associated damage; lower-grade high ankle sprain; mild hamstring strain or isolated low-grade MCL sprain; routine (non-syndesmotic) ankle sprain. This ordering is directional, not absolute — a recurrent hamstring issue can be more disruptive to a specific player's season than an ACL tear that occurred early enough in an offseason to allow a full rehabilitation window before the following season.

### Edge cases and known pitfalls

Sources converge on several recurring failure patterns. A compensatory injury cascade is commonly observed: a player protecting a healing joint or limb frequently overloads the opposite side, producing a documented pattern of soft-tissue strains on the healthy limb in the weeks following return from a major unilateral injury; a second ACL injury in particular is flagged as showing a meaningful tendency toward the contralateral (opposite) knee rather than the original surgical knee, a pattern that public analysis focused only on the originally-injured knee would miss entirely. Public injury labels are unreliable at the diagnostic level — "knee," "lower body," and "leg" can represent materially different underlying injuries, and two players sharing the same injury label (e.g., two hamstring strains) can have very different actual timelines depending on grade and location; sources also note that public "Grade 1" designations sometimes understate true severity relative to imaging findings, biasing public timelines optimistic by a week or more. Teams and public reporting frequently conflate return-to-play with return-to-production, creating a persistent public-optimism bias where a player being active is treated as equivalent to being fully restored. Older players are consistently flagged as recovering more slowly and retaining more persistent efficiency loss, particularly at running back and tight end, though sources do not converge on a specific quantified age interaction. Combined or concurrent injuries (for example, MCL and ACL damage occurring together) are flagged as requiring meaningfully longer timelines than either injury's isolated recovery window would suggest, because bracing and range-of-motion restrictions from one injury can delay rehabilitation progress on the other — a distinction sources note is often missed when public timelines default to the isolated-injury figure.

## Key Decisions

The platform will decompose injury-related projections into medical-return, functional-return, and fantasy-return stages rather than a single "healthy/injured" binary, because every sampled source independently identifies the collapse of these distinct thresholds into one as the most common and consequential injury-related projection error.

The platform will apply injury-type-specific recovery models (ACL, hamstring, high ankle sprain, MCL) rather than a generic "lower-body injury" discount, because sources converge that these four injury classes carry meaningfully different timelines, recurrence risk profiles, and position-sensitivity, and that treating them uniformly is a common and avoidable modeling error in existing roster tools.

The platform will treat the reported severity/timeline ranges in this page as directional priors with wide uncertainty bands rather than fixed point estimates, and will calibrate exact week-count parameters against the platform's own historical outcome data as it accumulates, because sources materially disagree on precise figures even while agreeing on relative ordering and directional patterns.

The platform will flag hamstring returns occurring within roughly two weeks of the original injury as elevated recurrence risk requiring a larger and more persistent projection discount than the raw "weeks missed" figure would suggest, because sources converge this is a well-documented and actionable pattern distinct from typical injury-severity discounting.

The platform will apply a larger and more sustained post-return discount for running backs and separation-dependent wide receivers than for pocket quarterbacks and possession-style pass catchers, holding injury type constant, because every source converges that lower-body injury classes disproportionately affect burst, cutting, and acceleration — the traits these positions and archetypes depend on most directly.
