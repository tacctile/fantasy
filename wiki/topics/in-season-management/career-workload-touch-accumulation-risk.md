## Summary

Cumulative career touches (carries plus receptions) function as a real risk amplifier for running back decline and injury, but only when combined with age, recent-season workload concentration, and injury history — five of six independently sampled models converge on this framing against one outlier model arguing career volume is primarily a "durability screening" signal (i.e., that surviving to high touch totals proves resilience rather than predicting breakdown). This page adopts the corroborated majority position: workload should be treated as a probabilistic prior that shifts confidence and discounts projections, never as a binary "washed" flag or a proof of durability. The strongest and most consistently corroborated finding across the panel is that raw career totals are the weakest form of this signal — age-interacted workload, workload concentration (recent, spiked, or repeated high-volume seasons), and injury history each add independent predictive value that a career-total-only model misses entirely.

## Core Knowledge

### Workload is a risk amplifier, not a standalone predictor — and one model's contrary view is a documented outlier

Five of six sampled models converge that cumulative career touches meaningfully raise the probability of running back injury, efficiency decline, or role loss, particularly when combined with advancing age. One model argued the opposite: that high-touch backs are a self-selected durable population (a "durability screening" effect) and that crossing career volume thresholds does not itself predict decline, citing peer-reviewed findings that some high-carry-season backs show fewer missed games and fewer concussions than moderate-usage peers. This page resolves the conflict in favor of the five-model corroboration, per the standard practice of weighing corroborated majority findings over a lone outlier, while preserving the outlier's most defensible sub-claim as a genuine caution: survivorship bias inflates the apparent safety of any historical high-touch cohort, because backs who could not tolerate that volume already exited the sample before reaching it. The practical synthesis is that career workload is a real risk signal that operates on the survivors who remain, not proof that touches themselves are protective.

### Age and cumulative workload interact multiplicatively, not additively

Every model that addressed the mechanism agrees that career touches and chronological age should not be scored as independent, additive risk factors — the same career touch total carries substantially higher risk for an older back than a younger one, because recovery capacity has already declined with age. A back who reaches a given cumulative touch total earlier in his career (via several high-volume seasons in rapid succession) is described across multiple models as carrying more risk than one who reaches the identical total gradually over many seasons, because the former accumulated the load without the recovery windows the latter received. This age-workload interaction is corroborated as the strongest single organizing principle in this domain, stronger than either variable alone, though no source converges on a precise numeric interaction coefficient — exact formulas offered by individual models are single-source constructs and are excluded from this synthesis as unverifiable.

### Recent and concentrated workload matters more than smooth career accumulation

Multiple models converge that workload concentration — a single extreme-volume season, or two-to-three consecutive high-volume seasons — carries more forward risk than the same cumulative total spread evenly across a long career. The historically cited "high single-season carry total creates elevated risk the following year" pattern (sometimes bundled under a specific numeric threshold in older public analysis) is treated across sources as directionally real but numerically unsettled: models disagree on the exact single-season or trailing-average threshold, and this page does not adopt any specific figure as settled. The corroborated, adoptable principle is relative, not absolute: prioritize trailing one-to-three-year workload and the presence of repeated high-volume seasons over the flat career total when assessing forward risk, and treat a single anomalous heavy season as less concerning than the same volume repeated in consecutive years.

### Touch types are not interchangeable, and receiving volume is not risk-free

Sources converge that carries and receptions likely carry different physical wear profiles, with most models treating interior/between-the-tackles carries as higher-impact than receptions, screens, or perimeter touches — though the panel does not converge on any specific weighting ratio between the two, and offered ratios varied widely enough across models to be treated as unverifiable single-source estimates rather than adopted figures. Multiple models independently flag that receiving-heavy backs are not protected from workload risk simply because their touches involve less between-the-tackles contact: open-field tackles and high-velocity collisions in space carry their own injury exposure, and at least one model specifically flagged that elite-volume receiving backs have shown their own soft-tissue injury patterns tied to receiving workload rather than being immune to accumulation risk. Pass-blocking snap exposure was independently raised by multiple models as a frequently omitted but plausible contributor to cumulative wear, since it involves high-force collisions that do not appear in any touch count at all — this is flagged as a probable blind spot in most public workload models rather than a settled quantified input.

### Injury history and injury type frequently outweigh raw career volume

Sources converge that prior injury history — particularly recurring soft-tissue injuries (hamstring, groin, calf) — is often a stronger and more immediately actionable risk signal than lifetime touch count alone, because recurring soft-tissue injury indicates the muscle-tendon system is already failing to recover between games, a more systemic signal than an accumulated touch count. Multiple sources specifically distinguish structural or single-event injuries (a fully healed ACL or ankle fracture) from chronic soft-tissue recurrence patterns, converging that the latter is frequently the more predictive signal for continued decline even when career touch totals are comparable. This is consistent with the injury-type-specific decomposition already established for post-injury projection generally: workload risk should be evaluated alongside injury type and recurrence pattern, not as a replacement for it.

### Career workload operates on three separable outcomes, not one

Models converge that "workload risk" is frequently treated as a single undifferentiated outcome when it should be decomposed into at least three distinct and separately trackable effects: probability of missing games (acute injury risk), probability of efficiency decline (reduced explosiveness, yards after contact, missed-tackles-forced, or explosive-play rate even while remaining available), and probability of role or opportunity loss (a team shifting touches to a younger or fresher back for reasons that may precede, follow, or occur independent of physical decline). Sources converge that efficiency decline and role decline frequently move on different timelines from each other and from acute injury — a back can remain fully available while quietly losing explosiveness, or can lose his role for organizational reasons that have nothing to do with demonstrated physical decline yet. Treating all three as one collapsed "washed" judgment is flagged as a common and avoidable error.

### Survivorship bias is the dominant methodological trap in this domain

Every source that addressed methodology flags survivorship bias as the central analytical hazard: running backs who accumulate very high career touch totals are, by definition, the subset who did not break down earlier, so any historical dataset of "backs at 2,000+ career touches" is already selected for durability and will understate the true risk faced by an average back approaching that volume. This cuts in a specific, non-obvious direction — it means the correct read of a historical high-touch cohort's apparent resilience is not "high volume is safe," but "these particular players survived long enough to prove they can tolerate volume the average back could not," which is precisely why the one-model "durability screening" thesis (see above) is only partially adoptable: it correctly identifies survivorship bias but draws the wrong inference from it, treating survival as evidence that workload doesn't cause decline rather than as a sampling artifact that hides the decline in players who already exited the pool.

### College workload is a plausible but unverified contributor to lifetime accumulation

Multiple models independently raised that college carries and receptions represent workload accumulated before NFL career touches begin, and that models starting the workload clock at the draft may understate true lifetime physical exposure for backs who carried a heavy three-or-four-year collegiate workload. There is no cross-model agreement on a conversion ratio between college and NFL touches (individual models offered specific multipliers, but these are single-source and excluded here as unverifiable), and this page treats college workload as a plausible secondary risk modifier worth qualitative awareness rather than a quantifiable input.

## Key Decisions

The platform will treat career workload as a risk-adjustment input to running back projections and dynasty valuation — widening the downside of the outcome distribution and increasing discount confidence — rather than as a binary "avoid" flag or a proof of proven durability, because the corroborated majority view across the panel treats workload as a probabilistic amplifier of risk, not a deterministic cliff, while the single-model "durability screening" counter-thesis is rejected as an outlier that also fails to account for survivorship bias correctly.

The platform will always interact career workload with player age when computing risk, rather than scoring either variable independently, because every model addressing mechanism converges that the same cumulative touch total carries materially different risk depending on the player's age and remaining recovery capacity.

The platform will weight recent and concentrated workload (trailing one-to-three-year totals, consecutive high-volume seasons) more heavily than smooth lifetime accumulation when assessing forward risk, because sources converge that workload concentration is a stronger signal than career totals alone, even though no specific numeric threshold is corroborated strongly enough to adopt as a fixed cutoff.

The platform will decompose workload risk into three separately tracked outcomes — availability risk, efficiency-decline risk, and role-loss risk — rather than a single undifferentiated "workload risk" score, because sources converge that these three outcomes move on different timelines and a collapsed single score obscures which risk is actually elevated for a given player.

The platform will weight recurring soft-tissue injury history at least as heavily as raw career touch totals when assessing running back risk, and will not treat a high career-touch total with a clean injury history as equivalent risk to a lower-touch player with recurring soft-tissue injuries, because sources converge that recurrence pattern is often the more directly predictive signal of the two.

The platform will not adopt any single-model numeric threshold for career touches, single-season touch counts, or college-to-NFL conversion ratios as a fixed modeling parameter, because the panel shows wide numeric disagreement on all such figures even while agreeing on the underlying directional principles — any such thresholds should be calibrated against the platform's own historical outcome data as it accumulates, consistent with the same approach already adopted for injury recovery timelines.

## Open Questions

- [ ] Whether the "durability screening" effect has any genuine validity for a subset of running back archetypes (e.g., contact-avoidant or low-BMI backs) even though it is rejected here as the dominant framework — flagged for future verification against a larger, more recent NFL sample.
- [ ] The correct relative weighting between carries, receptions, and pass-blocking snap exposure in a combined workload measure is not corroborated across sources and remains an open modeling question.
- [ ] Whether modern load-management practices (in-season rest, reduced practice reps, sports-science monitoring) have measurably shifted historical workload-risk thresholds for backs entering the league since roughly 2018 — raised across multiple sources as plausible but not resolved with corroborated evidence.
- [ ] Whether college workload should be incorporated into a lifetime touch count at all, and if so at what conversion weight — raised by multiple sources with no cross-model agreement on magnitude.
- [ ] The precise numeric threshold(s), if any, at which single-season or career touch accumulation meaningfully changes forward risk — sources agree directionally that concentration and volume matter but diverge too widely numerically to adopt any specific cutoff; flagged in `wiki/verification-cache.md` as pending independent verification.
