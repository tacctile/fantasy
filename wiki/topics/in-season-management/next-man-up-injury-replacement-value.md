## Summary

All six independently sampled models converge that next-man-up analysis is fundamentally a role-inheritance problem, not a depth-chart-proximity or talent-evaluation problem: the correct question is which specific components of an injured player's workload (carries, routes, targets, red-zone touches, third-down/two-minute usage, pass-blocking assignments) transfer to which teammate, not simply "who is the backup." Carry-over rate is corroborated as strongly position-dependent — highest and most direct for running backs, most fragile for wide receivers because vacated targets condense toward already-trusted players rather than transferring to the nominal backup, and lowest for tight ends unless the replacement already had a route-heavy profile before the injury. Matchup quality and raw efficiency metrics are corroborated as second-order modifiers that should be applied only after role and opportunity are established, and the highest-value acquisition window is immediately after injury news breaks, before coaching-staff language or committee declarations move the market.

## Core Knowledge

### Role decomposition, not depth-chart order, is the corroborated unit of analysis

Every source independently rejects the framing of next-man-up as "who is the #2 on the depth chart" and instead decomposes the injured player's role into distinct opportunity categories that do not necessarily transfer to the same player: early-down carries, short-yardage/goal-line carries, receiving-down carries and targets, two-minute/hurry-up snaps, pass-blocking assignments, and — for receivers and tight ends — routes run, first-read/primary-option designation, slot versus perimeter alignment, red-zone routes, and designed/manufactured touches. Sources converge that a single aggregate "carry-over rate" applied uniformly across all of these categories is a common and material source of projection error; target carry-over, route carry-over, red-zone carry-over, and goal-line carry-over should be estimated and tracked separately because they frequently diverge sharply from one another for the same injury event.

### Carry-over rate is position-specific and converges on a consistent ordering across sources

Running backs show the highest and most direct carry-over when a clear lead back is injured, with cited ranges clustering around 0.6–0.85 for early-down and rushing volume in a stable, non-committee offensive structure. This is corroborated as the most reliable positional carry-over pattern, but sources caution it is conditional on the backup's pass-protection competence and the coaching staff's trust in that specific skill — a backup earning early-down carries does not automatically inherit third-down or two-minute work, and in PPR formats that split matters more than the aggregate touch count suggests. Wide receiver carry-over is corroborated as meaningfully lower and more fragile, commonly cited in the 0.2–0.7 range depending on source and offensive context, because vacated targets are frequently redistributed across the existing receiving corps toward already-trusted players (the alpha receiver, the slot receiver, a receiving back) rather than transferring cleanly to the direct backup — sources describe this as an "empty routes" or "empty volume" trap, where a backup inherits route participation but not the target-earning share that made the original role valuable. Tight end carry-over is corroborated as the lowest and most conditional of the three, because tight end depth charts are frequently split functionally between receiving specialists, inline blockers, and special-teams players; carry-over is strong only when the replacement already demonstrated meaningful route participation and target-earning before the injury, and weak or negligible when the backup's pre-injury role was primarily blocking or special teams. Quarterback injury replacement is corroborated as structurally different and generally negative for the offense as a whole — the relevant question for skill-position players shifts from "is the backup good" to which specific throw types and tendencies survive the change (deep shots, anticipatory intermediate throws, and red-zone efficiency are commonly reduced; checkdowns, scramble-drill production, and condensed first-read throws commonly increase), meaning a backup quarterback's impact must be modeled as a change in offensive structure, not merely a talent downgrade.

### Matchup quality and raw efficiency are modifiers, applied after role and opportunity, not before

Sources converge that immediate matchup quality — typically operationalized as recent (3-5 week) position-specific fantasy points allowed cross-referenced with the offense's Vegas-implied team total and game script expectation — should scale a role-and-opportunity projection rather than generate one independently. A player projected for a narrow, low-leverage role does not become a strong play solely because the matchup is favorable; conversely a strong role projection can be meaningfully discounted by a difficult matchup or a low implied team total that suppresses scoring opportunity independent of volume. Raw per-touch efficiency metrics for the replacement (yards per carry, missed tackles forced, yards per route run, and similar) are corroborated as lower-confidence inputs in the first one to two weeks after an injury because they are typically drawn from small, low-volume, favorable-situation samples that do not reliably predict performance once workload scales up.

### Common failure patterns cluster around premature certainty and structural blind spots

Sources converge on several recurring pitfalls. First, assuming a single player inherits the entire vacated role when the coaching staff instead deploys a committee — splitting early-down, passing-down, and goal-line work across multiple players — is repeatedly cited as the most common and costly analytical error, particularly at running back. Second, a public "we'll use multiple guys" or similarly hedged coaching statement is frequently a coach's hedge rather than a firm usage plan; sources diverge on how strongly to weight this language but converge that it should not be treated as a reliable usage forecast without confirming game-tape evidence of actual rotation. Third, offensive-line health is an underweighted modifier — a replacement player, especially at running back, operating behind a compromised offensive line is materially less valuable than the same role behind a healthy line, and this factor is frequently absent from public waiver-wire discourse. Fourth, the first game after an injury can reflect an improvised, emergency game plan rather than the stable go-forward role; a second consecutive game of confirming usage is a stronger signal than a single post-injury performance. Fifth, quiet or secondary injuries to complementary pieces (another receiver, the starting quarterback, a lead blocker) can silently erase or redirect the apparent opportunity created by the primary injury and must be checked before assuming a clean role transfer. Sixth, a change in offensive scheme or personnel usage (for example, shifting to more multi-tight-end formations, shorter passing concepts, or additional max-protect looks) can substitute for a like-for-like player replacement, meaning the offense itself changes rather than one player simply stepping into another's shoes.

### Acquisition timing and roster-construction guidance

Sources converge with high confidence that the highest-value acquisition window is immediate — the period between injury confirmation and the first subsequent coach press conference or practice report — because waiver priority, FAAB pricing, and trade value all move quickly once public role clarity increases, and waiting for full clarity typically means competing on equal information with the rest of the league. Sources also converge that one-week streaming value and rest-of-season stash value are distinct evaluations that should not be conflated: a player representing the best immediate matchup-driven start is not necessarily the best asset to roster long-term, and vice versa. High-value usage types — targets, goal-line touches, red-zone involvement, and two-minute/hurry-up snaps — are corroborated as more predictive of sustained fantasy value than raw neutral-field carry volume, meaning a replacement with a smaller but higher-leverage role can outproduce one with a larger but lower-leverage role, particularly in PPR-scoring formats.

## Key Decisions

The platform will decompose an injured player's role into distinct opportunity categories (early-down carries, short-yardage/goal-line work, receiving-down usage, two-minute/third-down snaps, and — for pass catchers — routes, target share, red-zone routes, and slot/perimeter alignment) and estimate carry-over separately for each category rather than applying a single aggregate carry-over rate, because sources converge that these categories frequently diverge sharply for the same injury event and that aggregate rates are a primary source of projection error.

The platform will apply position-specific default carry-over priors (highest and most direct for running backs, lower and conditional on target-earning ability for wide receivers, lowest and conditional on pre-injury route participation for tight ends) as a starting point for replacement-value projections, adjusting for role fit and offensive-line health, because these positional orderings are consistently corroborated across sources even though exact numerical rates vary.

The platform will treat matchup quality and per-touch efficiency metrics as modifiers applied after an opportunity/role projection is established, not as standalone drivers of a next-man-up recommendation, because sources converge that role and volume are the dominant variables and that early-sample efficiency metrics are unreliable at low workload.

The platform will flag committee risk (multiple functionally distinct roles historically split across more than one player on a given depth chart) as a confidence downgrade on any single-player replacement projection, and will require confirming game-tape or multi-game usage evidence before treating a coach's "we'll use multiple guys" statement as either confirmed or refuted, because sources converge that coach language alone is an unreliable usage predictor.

The platform will surface acquisition-priority alerts immediately upon confirmed injury news rather than waiting for full depth-chart clarity, and will separately label one-week streaming recommendations from rest-of-season stash recommendations, because sources converge that acquisition speed captures the largest available edge and that these two use cases require different evaluation criteria.

## Open Questions

- [ ] Whether observed carry-over rates reflect a causal role-transfer effect or selection bias (teams and coaching staffs that develop clear positional hierarchies may also be better-coached teams generally) is raised without a corroborated resolution across sources.
- [ ] The optimal number of confirming games (one versus two) required before treating a post-injury role as stable rather than an emergency/improvised plan is raised across sources without a single corroborated threshold, and should be treated as a tunable parameter alongside the multi-week confirmation threshold used for general role-change detection.
- [ ] Whether team-context signals (offensive line injury status, secondary skill-position injuries, quarterback change) should be modeled as a combined joint-probability adjustment rather than sequential independent checks is raised as a plausible refinement without a validated methodology in the source material.
