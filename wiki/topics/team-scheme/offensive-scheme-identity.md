## Summary

Offensive scheme identity — the coach-lineage label a team carries (Shanahan/wide-zone, Air Raid/spread, West Coast, Coryell/vertical, spread-option/RPO, Erhardt-Perkins) — is a probability modifier on player opportunity, not a projection substitute. Every model in this synthesis converges on the same core mechanism: scheme sets a structural prior on which positions are advantaged, but observed, current-season functional tendencies (personnel usage, PROE, motion rate, play-action rate, route concepts, actual run-blocking scheme) determine real opportunity far more reliably than the coaching-tree label itself. The dominant failure mode across all six models is treating heritage or coach biography as a stable, portable predictor after a coordinator, quarterback, or personnel change.

## Core Knowledge

### The Scheme Families and Their Structural Biases

Six functional archetypes recur across all six model responses with consistent position-level implications:

- **Shanahan/McVay wide-zone systems** — outside-zone/stretch runs paired with complementary bootleg play-action off identical run-look formations. Structurally advantages one-cut, vision-based running backs (not necessarily power runners), Y-tight ends running deep crossers, and receivers who win off play-action leverage created by motion. Does not guarantee a bell-cow — the run scheme can be excellent while touches are still split by down/role (see backfield committee structure).
- **Air Raid / spread-passing systems** — shotgun-based, minimal or eliminated in-line tight end usage, 10/11 personnel, spacing-based route concepts (Mesh, Y-Cross, Four Verticals, Stick). Structurally elevates slot receiver and overall WR target volume and pass attempt counts; typically suppresses traditional in-line tight end target share unless the offense deliberately funnels a big-slot type player there.
- **West Coast / timing-and-spacing systems** — short-to-intermediate timing routes, progression-read passing, high completion rate. The label covers materially different offenses in practice; it does not by itself determine whether targets concentrate on one receiver or distribute across multiple route-tree specialists — that outcome depends on the specific progression structure and quarterback tendency layered on top.
- **Coryell / vertical systems** — deep play-action shots (posts, seams, flies) with power/gap run games underneath. Structurally advantages boundary X-receivers, pass-catching tight ends running seam routes, and deep-threat receivers.
- **Spread-option / RPO-heavy systems** — post-snap run/pass reads tied to a single conflict defender. Structurally elevates mobile quarterback rushing value and slot/quick-game receiver volume; structurally suppresses traditional running back receiving work (the quarterback keeps or throws the bubble/screen instead of checking down) and compresses RB goal-line share when the quarterback is a real short-yardage rushing threat.
- **Erhardt-Perkins / game-plan-specific systems** — no fixed weekly identity; plays are named by formation-plus-concept and the offense adapts opponent-to-opponent. Structurally advantages positionally versatile players who create mismatches through alignment, and produces genuinely unpredictable week-to-week target distribution that resists scheme-label-based projection.

Most current NFL offenses are hybrids of these families. A label typically describes the dominant stress point of an offense, not its entire operation, and defensive convergence toward light, split-safety shells has pushed offensive systems to blend wide-zone runs with spread/RPO elements, further shrinking the real variance between labeled systems.

### Quantifying Identity: Functional Metrics Over Labels

All six models converge that scheme should be measured through observed, situation-adjusted behavior rather than assigned by coach biography:

- **Pass Rate Over Expected (PROE)** — actual pass rate minus a model-expected pass rate given down, distance, field position, score, and time. The most portable and stable cross-team, cross-season scheme signal because it isolates play-calling aggressiveness from the game states a schedule happened to produce.
- **Personnel grouping frequency** (11/12/21/10) — sets the structural floor/ceiling on which positions can be on the field for a given play; it does not by itself confirm route participation or touch rate within that package.
- **Motion and shift rate** — pre-snap movement rate, concentrated in wide-zone/McVay-lineage systems, that distorts defensive run-fits and forces zone defenders to pass off assignments, inflating both yards per carry and yards per route run for the offense independent of pure talent.
- **Play-action rate and RPO rate** — both reliably boost quarterback efficiency by manipulating second-level defenders; RPO plays additionally shift designed touches between run and pass options based on a post-snap defender read.
- **Neutral-situation pace and neutral pass rate** — plays per game and pass rate filtered to competitive score states (commonly within one score, first three quarters), removing garbage-time inflation and lead-protecting run-out distortion.
- **Run concept taxonomy** — actual zone-versus-gap blocking-scheme frequency, which is more predictive for running back fit (one-cut vision runner versus power/gap runner) than the team's overall scheme label.

A basic fantasy-usable opportunity model that appears in some form across all six responses:

$$\text{Player Fantasy Expectation} = \text{Team Plays} \times \text{Player Opportunity Rate} \times \text{Efficiency} \times \text{Scoring Value}$$

Scheme primarily moves the opportunity-rate and efficiency-distribution terms; quarterback quality, offensive-line quality, coaching, injuries, and game script affect all four terms simultaneously, which is why isolating a pure "scheme effect" from public data is difficult.

### Edge Cases, Failure Patterns, and Pitfalls

- **The "chameleon coordinator."** Some play-callers carry no fixed identity and shift scheme family repeatedly within a season based on opponent game-plan (documented as a repeated, non-hypothetical pattern). Projecting from career or prior-team history fails against this coordinator type; rolling 3-6 week observed tendency is the correction all six models converge on.
- **Personnel-versus-scheme mismatch.** A team may carry a scheme label (e.g., "wide-zone") while its actual roster forces a different style — a power-running back miscast into a zone scheme, or a poor-blocking offensive line unable to execute the labeled run concept, producing an inefficient hybrid that invalidates historical coordinator-tendency data.
- **Quarterback override.** A mobile or highly improvisational quarterback can override a designed rhythm-and-timing system (high scramble rate, high aDOT relative to system baseline, low checkdown rate), or conversely a slow-processing quarterback can cause a timing-dependent scheme to collapse into a scramble-drill offense — the 2022 Broncos under a West Coast-lineage staff with a quarterback unable to execute the timing structure is cited as a canonical real-world case of scheme abandonment by midseason.
- **Coach-tree assumptions are weaker than commonly assumed.** Coaches sharing a lineage (e.g., multiple Shanahan-tree hires) can diverge substantially in personnel usage and target distribution even while sharing broad structural DNA (outside zone, boot-action play-pass concepts); tree lineage predicts structure, not detailed tendency profiles.
- **Defensive in-season adaptation.** Concepts that work in the season's first quarter are frequently schematically countered by opposing defensive coordinators later in the season (e.g., defenses learning to spill and contain a boot-action leak concept), which changes running back and tight end opportunity mid-season independent of any change in the offense's own personnel or design.
- **Static labeling of evolving systems.** Applying a single fixed scheme label across a full season, or across a coordinator change, ignores in-season installation periods (the first several games of a new play-caller's tenure reflect installation and personnel discovery, not settled philosophy) and mid-season adaptation.

### Platform and Provider Differences

- **Coach-lineage labels** (used by general fantasy sites and much mainstream coverage) categorize by coaching pedigree and historical association; useful for a rough opportunity-shape prior but frequently lag actual in-season play design and can obscure a genuine dominant concept within a team labeled ambiguously as "multiple."
- **Personnel and formation charting providers** (tracking 11/12/21 usage, motion rate, shotgun rate) offer the most transparent and independently verifiable proxy for scheme identity, but do not resolve intent — a nominal personnel package label does not update when pre-snap motion changes the functional alignment.
- **Concept- and route-charting providers** classify play-action, RPO, and route-family frequency through manual charting judgment; these classifications are not standardized across providers, particularly for broken-down plays where a designed pass becomes a scramble, or an RPO resolves as a handoff — different charting conventions credit these plays differently, which can shift a derived scheme-identity metric by several percentage points.
- **No provider publishes a universally accepted, fantasy-specific taxonomy** that converts a scheme-family label directly into position-level opportunity multipliers; any such multiplier in circulation is an analyst-constructed prior, not an industry standard.

## Key Decisions

- **Decision:** The platform will treat offensive scheme identity as a low-weight prior applied before observed, current-season personnel and tendency data, and will re-weight aggressively toward observed data as soon as a meaningful in-season sample (roughly 4-8 games) accumulates.
  **Reasoning:** All six models converge that coach-lineage labels lag actual play design and that personnel, PROE, motion rate, and route-participation data are more reliable and more current signals of real opportunity than heritage-based classification.
  **Rejected alternative:** Assigning a fixed scheme-family label per team and applying static position-level multipliers for the full season was rejected because it fails against documented in-season coordinator adaptation, personnel-scheme mismatches, and defensive counter-adjustment, all of which are established, recurring patterns rather than edge cases.

- **Decision:** The platform will decompose scheme identity into its component functional signals (PROE, personnel frequency, motion rate, play-action rate, RPO rate, neutral pace) rather than displaying a single coach-lineage label as an opportunity signal.
  **Reasoning:** Every model treats the label itself as too coarse to drive player-level projection; the underlying functional metrics are what actually move opportunity, and they can move independently of, or even contrary to, the nominal scheme label.
  **Rejected alternative:** Surfacing only a single scheme-family tag per team (e.g., "Shanahan-style") was rejected as analytically insufficient and potentially misleading given documented divergence within the same coaching tree.

- **Decision:** The platform will flag quarterback style (mobility, processing speed, scramble rate) as an explicit override variable on any scheme-based opportunity projection, rather than assuming scheme dictates player role independent of the quarterback on the roster.
  **Reasoning:** Quarterback override is identified across responses as one of the most consistent scheme-identity failure patterns — a system's theoretical opportunity distribution can collapse or invert based on whether the quarterback can execute the scheme's core reads.
  **Rejected alternative:** Modeling scheme-driven opportunity independent of quarterback fit was rejected because documented cases (system-quarterback mismatches producing scheme abandonment) show the interaction is not a minor adjustment but can dominate the outcome.
