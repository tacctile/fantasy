## Summary

Stacking pairs a quarterback with one or more of his own pass-catchers (or, in a "bring-back," a pass-catcher from the opposing team) to introduce positive score correlation into a roster, raising the probability of an extreme high-scoring week without raising either player's individual expected points. The value comes entirely from the covariance term in the roster's combined-score variance, not from any change to the underlying projections — which is why stacking is a near-mandatory construction tool in best ball and DFS tournament formats that reward ceiling outcomes, but only a secondary tiebreaker consideration in season-long managed redraft, where median weekly scoring matters more than tail outcomes. The most consistently corroborated failure pattern is forcing a stack (reaching for a weaker pass-catcher, or concentrating too many roster spots in one offense) when the correlation gain is smaller than the value given up.

## Core Knowledge

### Stacking is a variance tool, not an expected-value tool

Rostering a quarterback together with a pass-catcher on the same team creates positive correlation because a large share of the pass-catcher's fantasy production is directly generated by the quarterback's passing output — completions, yardage, and touchdowns. If the two players' projections are unchanged, stacking them does not change the sum of their expected points; the advantage comes entirely from the covariance term in the combined-score variance, which raises the probability of an extreme high-scoring outcome in weeks where the offense performs well, while lowering it in weeks where the offense performs poorly. This is corroborated as the single most important conceptual distinction in evaluating stacks: correlation is not a substitute for individual projection quality, and a poorly projected quarterback-receiver pair remains a poor pairing regardless of how tightly correlated their outcomes are.

### Format determines whether stacking is mandatory, optional, or irrelevant

In best ball and large-field tournament formats, stacking is close to a required construction element because these formats reward extreme high-scoring outcomes over median or consistent production — the platform automatically captures a roster's ceiling weeks without requiring a lineup decision, so concentrating correlated upside directly increases the probability of a winning score. In DFS tournaments the incentive is even stronger and stacking is treated as close to mandatory by most serious builders, because top-heavy payout structures require reaching the extreme tail of the score distribution to win anything meaningful. In season-long managed redraft, the incentive is weaker: a manager only needs to outscore a single opponent each week, so higher variance is not automatically desirable, and forcing a stack at the expense of a clearly better individual player is a documented value-destroying mistake. The most defensible use of stacking in redraft is as a tiebreaker between two similarly valued players, or as a deliberate choice late in a season when trailing in the standings and needing higher-variance, ceiling-chasing rosters to catch up.

### Quarterback plus primary pass-catcher is the most reliable stack structure

A quarterback paired with his clear primary target — the receiver or tight end with the largest share of targets and red-zone opportunity — is the most consistently corroborated correlation structure, because his production is most directly tied to the quarterback's overall passing success. Stack quality depends heavily on which pass-catcher is chosen: a receiver with concentrated target share and red-zone involvement has direct touchdown correlation and the highest stack ceiling; a deep-threat receiver has strong touchdown correlation but more volatile week-to-week target volume; a possession receiver correlates through yardage and targets but typically has a lower touchdown ceiling; a tight end can be a strong stack partner if he holds a dominant share of intermediate and red-zone targets, but is a weak stack partner otherwise. Pairing a quarterback with a low-volume or replaceable pass-catcher produces minimal correlation benefit while still incurring the roster cost of concentrating draft capital in one offense.

### Double and triple stacks increase both ceiling and concentration risk

Adding a second or third pass-catcher from the same offense to a quarterback stack increases the roster's correlated ceiling further, but also increases the risk that a single bad week for that offense collapses multiple roster slots simultaneously. This concentration risk is corroborated as a genuine failure pattern distinct from the general value of stacking — a double or triple stack is best reserved for offenses with unusually high projected pass volume and multiple clearly concentrated roles, not applied indiscriminately as "more stacking is always better." A quarterback with substantial rushing production has a weaker case for a large stack, because a meaningful share of his fantasy value comes from designed runs and scrambles that do not generate any corresponding production for his pass-catchers — a mobile quarterback's rushing floor is largely independent of the receiving corps, which somewhat dilutes the correlation benefit relative to a high-volume pocket passer.

### Bring-back stacks diversify correlation across two offenses

A bring-back adds a pass-catcher from the opposing team to a quarterback-receiver stack, on the logic that a competitive, high-scoring game forces both offenses to maintain aggressive passing volume, extending the correlated upside beyond a single team. This is most relevant in tournament and DFS formats seeking maximum game-environment exposure; it is not a requirement for every stack, and a one-sided game can still produce excellent quarterback and receiver scores if the passing offense stays aggressive independent of the score. Forcing a bring-back purely to complete a stack, when the opposing pass-catcher lacks independent value at his draft or roster cost, repeats the same value-destroying mistake as reaching for a weak same-team pass-catcher.

### Running backs on the stacked team carry mixed, context-dependent correlation

A team's running back is not automatically a clean addition to a quarterback-receiver stack. Rushing touchdowns can directly substitute for passing touchdowns on the same set of scoring drives, creating some negative correlation between a team's lead running back and its quarterback's passing output; however, a genuinely high-scoring team can still support strong performances from both the passing attack and the running game simultaneously. This relationship is context- and role-dependent rather than a fixed rule, and should not be treated as either reliably positive or reliably negative without accounting for the specific offense's scoring patterns and the running back's role in the passing game.

### Scoring rules shift which stack elements matter most

Six-point passing-touchdown scoring shifts relatively more value to the quarterback within a stack, since he captures a larger share of the touchdown value than his pass-catchers; four-point passing-touchdown scoring shifts relative value toward the pass-catchers, whose touchdown and reception scoring make up a larger share of the stack's total output. Full-PPR scoring increases the value of high-target-volume pass-catchers in a stack regardless of touchdown production, while touchdown-dependent or big-play pass-catchers gain relative value in lower-reception-value formats. Tight end premium scoring can make quarterback-tight end stacks more competitive with quarterback-wide receiver stacks than they would be under standard scoring.

## Key Decisions

The platform will model stack value as a variance/covariance effect layered on top of independent player projections, not as an adjustment to either player's individual expected points, because sources consistently and strongly corroborate that stacking raises ceiling probability without changing the underlying sum of expected value.

The platform will scale stacking guidance by contest format — treating it as a near-required construction element in best ball and tournament contexts, and as a secondary tiebreaker in season-long managed redraft — because sources consistently distinguish these as having fundamentally different incentive structures (ceiling-maximizing versus median-outscoring-an-opponent).

The platform will prioritize primary, high-target-share, red-zone-involved pass-catchers as the default stack partner recommendation over low-volume or deep-threat-only options, because sources agree correlation strength depends heavily on target concentration and touchdown involvement, not merely being on the same roster as the quarterback.

The platform will flag double and triple same-team stacks as a distinct, higher-concentration-risk construction choice rather than treating additional stacking as strictly additive value, because sources consistently identify over-concentration in a single offense as a documented failure pattern independent of the general value of stacking.

The platform will not treat bring-back stacking as a required stack component; it will be surfaced as an optional format-dependent enhancement, because sources agree a one-sided game can still produce strong stack outcomes and forcing a bring-back purely for structural completeness has been identified as a value-destroying pattern.

## Open Questions

- [ ] How much observed stacking success reflects genuine correlation benefit versus simply selecting strong players from strong offenses in the first place — sources note this confound but do not resolve it, and it would need isolated backtesting controlling for player quality independent of stack structure.
- [ ] What the optimal maximum number of same-team players to roster before concentration risk outweighs correlation benefit is — sources give inconsistent estimates and this likely varies by format and payout structure.
- [ ] Whether the correlation benefit of a running back within a quarterback-led stack is net positive or net negative in typical offenses — sources describe the relationship as context-dependent without a settled general rule.
